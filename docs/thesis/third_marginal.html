44  🚧 Inspecting the Margin(al)s

Having established that LGCP and CSCP models can exhibit nearly identical second-order structure, we now ask: where do these models differ?

Our first foray into non-first- and second-order statistics begins with the marginal distributions of the latent fields. While the pair correlation function captures second-order dependence, it does not fully characterise the distribution of \(\Lambda(u)\) at a fixed location.

Importantly: although the latent intensity is not directly observed, it still determines the local behaviour of the process, and therefore influences observable quantities such as counts in small regions. The hope is that the differences are large enough that they are able to survive what is becoming increasingly apparent as a total nuclear homogenizing poisson sampling step.

The most obvious way to start investigating the difference is to plot the two processes marginal intensity distributions, for a fixed \((\lambda, \phi)\), and obviously using the best-matching scale parameters (now easily calculatable using the amazing match_lgcp_scale() function).

I don’t know why I thought we needed to vary the scale parameters here. As we will see below, the scale has no effect on the marginal distribution. Reason being: the scale describes the decay of the spatial dependence. The marginal distribution is for a single location, no spatial dependence is involved.

44.1 Recapping

So before we cover the differences in marginal distributions, it is probably a good idea for us to revise what the marginal distributions even are.

So recall, a cox process is defined by it’s random intensity function \(\Lambda(u)\).

Now, this random intensity function is a Random Field, meaning that each location \(u\) over which \(\Lambda\) is defined, is distributed according to the full joint distribution specified by \(\Lambda\).

What does this actually mean though?

Well, \(\Lambda(u)\) is essentially assigning a random distribution to each one of the uncountably infinite number of locations over which it is defined, and the exact distributions being assigned are determined by the functional form and parameters of \(\Lambda\).

In the case of the LGCP (and CSCP), the random field \(\Lambda\), is a special kind of random field called a Gaussian Random Field. This means that if we consider any finite set of locations, say \(\{u_1, u_2, u_3, \dots, u_n\}\), then they will be distributed according to a multivariate normal distribution (with WHAT parameters?).

So you could imagine drawing an infinite number of random distributions, positioned vertically at each location, and transforming them according to whatever functional form \(\Lambda\) takes. This is exactly what \(\Lambda\) represents. An inifite number of possible inhomogeneous intensity surfaces.

Now, what we have described is the full joint-distribution of \(\Lambda\). But, this is a very difficult object to estimate for observed point patterns.

If we want to find differences between two \(\Lambda\)’s, and we can directly observe, or even estimate it well, then perhaps we could look at some properties of the \(\Lambda\)’s, and if the \(\Lambda\)’s we are comparing are sufficiently different, then we might be able to tell them apart (poorly phrased).

One example of this we saw in the last few chapters, where the “property” we were looking at was the PCF, a summary of essentially the covariance between two locations.

This section, we are considering a different “property”, that is the marginal distribution of the \(\Lambda\)’s.

So how exactly are the marginal distributions different than the full joint distribution?

Essentially, we are “accounting for” all possible values of the other locations and taking the correlation of them into account, and “averaging out” the effects due to the correlation / covariance.

The marginal distribution is essentially telling us: in the complete absence of information about any other locations, what would the distribution of the intensity look like at this single point.

In other words: If we repeatedly resampled from just this single point, after resampling the entire surface \(\Lambda\) many times, what would the distribution of the intensity surfaces look like?

44.2 Recap - corrected

So before we cover the differences in marginal distributions, it is probably a good idea for us to revise what the marginal distributions even are.

Recall, a cox process is defined by its random intensity function \(\Lambda(u)\).

This is a function which takes a location \(u\) as an argument, and outputs a random variable, which describes the intensity of the field at that location.

The form \(\Lambda\) takes, is a transformation of some Random Field.

Note that both LGCP and CSCP transform Gaussian random fields. GRFs are random fields such that any finite subset of them will be multivariate-normally distributed.

Now note, \(\Lambda(u)\) determines the full joint distribution of the Cox Process, over all locations. This means (better would be: can be thought of as…) it is essentially responsible for assigning a random variable to each one of the uncountably infinite number of locations over which it is defined, and the exact distributions of these RVs being assigned are determined by the functional form and parameters of \(\Lambda\).

This full joint distribution is a very difficult object to estimate from just observed point patterns.

So if we want to find differences between two \(\Lambda\)’s, and we can’t directly observe, or even estimate them well, then perhaps we could look at some properties of the \(\Lambda\)’s, and if the \(\Lambda\)’s we are comparing are sufficiently different, then we might be able to tell them apart (poorly phrased).

One example of this we saw in the last few chapters, where the “property” we were looking at was the PCF, a summary of essentially the covariance between two locations.

This section, we are considering a different “property”, that is the marginal distribution of the \(\Lambda\)’s.

So how exactly are the marginal distributions different than the full joint distribution?

Essentially, we are “accounting for” all possible values of the other locations and taking the correlation of them into account, and integrating out the dependence on all other locations.

The marginal distribution is essentially telling us: in the complete absence of information about any other locations, what would the distribution of the intensity look like at this single point.

In other words, if we repeatedly simulated the full intensity surface \(\Lambda\), and recorded the value at a fixed location \(u\), the resulting values would follow the marginal distribution of \(\Lambda(u)\).

44.4 True marginals

Obvious first step. Derive these bad boys. Starting with the CSCP.

44.4.1 Marginal distribution of the CSCP

Recall the definition of our beautiful CSCP:

\[ \Lambda(u) = \mu + Z(u)^2, \quad Z(u) \sim N(0, \sigma^2) \]

Fix a location \(u\). Since

\[ Z(u) \sim N(0, \sigma^2) \]

we can standardise, to obtain:

\[ \frac{Z(u)}{\sigma} \sim N(0, 1) \]

Now squaring both sides, we get:

\[ (\frac{Z(u)}{\sigma})^2 \sim \chi_1^2 \]

since the square of a standard normal RV is chi-square with 1 df.

So we have:

\[ \frac{Z(u)^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi_1^2 \]

Now, recall that

\[ \Lambda(u) = \mu + Z(u)^2 \]

so

\[ \Lambda(u) - \mu = Z(u)^2 \Longrightarrow \frac{\Lambda(u) - \mu}{\sigma^2} = \frac{Z(u)^2}{\sigma^2} \]

And using the above we have:

\[ \frac{\Lambda(u) - \mu}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi_1^2 \]

OK cool, so now what?

Let

\[ Y = \frac{\Lambda(u) - \mu}{\sigma^2} \]

Then, from above we have \(Y \sim \chi_1^2\) and also

\[ \Lambda(u) = \mu + \sigma^2 \Big(\frac{\Lambda(u) - \mu}{\sigma^2}\Big) = \mu + \sigma^2Y \]

And so we can use the change-of-variable formula:

\[ f_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = f_Y(\frac{\lambda - \mu}{\sigma^2})|\frac{d}{d\lambda}\Big( \frac{\lambda - \mu}{\sigma^2}\Big)| \]

and since

\[ \frac{d}{d\lambda}\Big( \frac{\lambda - \mu}{\sigma^2}\Big) = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \]

we obtain

\[ f_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} f_{\chi_1^2}\Big( \frac{\lambda - \mu}{\sigma^2}\Big), \quad \lambda > \mu \]

Also since \(Z(u)^2 \ge 0\), we must have \(\Lambda(u) \ge \mu\), so the density is zero for \(\lambda \le \mu\).

Now, if we want to be extra explicit, we have:

\[ f_{\chi_1^2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}x^{-1/2}e^{-x/2},\quad x > 0 \]

and so subbing this in we get:

\[ f_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\Big( \frac{\lambda - \mu}{\sigma^2}\Big)^{-1/2}\exp\Big(- \frac{\lambda - \mu}{2\sigma^2}\Big), \quad \lambda > \mu \]

Or equivalently:

\[ f_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \sigma^2 (\lambda - \mu)}}\exp\Big(- \frac{\lambda - \mu}{2\sigma^2}\Big), \quad \lambda > \mu \]

So, at a fixed location \(u\), the marginal distribution follows a shifted and scaled Chi-square.

In particular:

  • it has support over \((\mu, \infty)\)

  • explodes to infinity (?) as \(\lambda \to \mu\)

  • Right tail decays exponentially

44.4.2 Marginal distribution of the LGCP

Now recall the definition of the LGCP:

\[ \Lambda(u) = \exp(Z(u)), \quad Z(u) \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2) \]

Fix a location \(u\). Since

\[ Z(u) \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2) \]

and

\[ \Lambda(u) = \exp(Z(u)) \]

we can take logs of both sides to obtain

\[ \log \Lambda(u) = Z(u) \]

But then, since \(Z(u) \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\), we immediately have

\[ \log \Lambda(u) \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2) \]

And this is exactly the definition of a lognormal random variable. Hence

\[ \Lambda(u) \sim \operatorname{Lognormal}(\mu, \sigma^2) \]

OK cool, so now what?

If we want to write down the density explicitly, let

\[ Y = Z(u) \]

Then

\[ Y \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2) \]

and also

\[ \Lambda(u) = e^Y \]

Equivalently,

\[ Y = \log \Lambda(u) \]

So we can use the change-of-variable formula:

\[ f_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = f_Y(\log \lambda)\Big|\frac{d}{d\lambda}(\log \lambda)\Big|, \qquad \lambda > 0 \]

Now since

\[ \frac{d}{d\lambda}(\log \lambda) = \frac{1}{\lambda} \]

we obtain

\[ f_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = \frac{1}{\lambda} f_{N(\mu,\sigma^2)}(\log \lambda), \qquad \lambda > 0 \]

Now, if we want to be extra explicit, recall that the density of a normal random variable is

\[ f_{N(\mu,\sigma^2)}(y) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\Big( -\frac{(y-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \Big), \quad y \in \mathbb{r echo = FALSE} \]

So substituting \(y = \log \lambda\), we get

\[ f_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = \frac{1}{\lambda} \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\Big( -\frac{(\log \lambda - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \Big), \quad \lambda > 0 \]

That is,

\[ f_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = \frac{1}{\lambda \sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\Big( -\frac{(\log \lambda - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \Big), \quad \lambda > 0 \]

Also, since \(\Lambda(u)=\exp(Z(u)) > 0\) always, the density is zero for \(\lambda \le 0\).

So, at a fixed location \(u\), the marginal intensity follows a lognormal random variable.

In particular:

  • it has support over \((0, \infty)\)

  • tends to \(0\) as \(\lambda \to 0\)

  • has a heavier right tail than the CSCP marginal

44.6 Maybe CDF is a little nicer?

Corresponding cumulative distribution functions follow immediately from the transformed representations used above.

Hopefully useful as: firstly, they provide a cumulative description of the one-point marginal laws. secondly, they will later allow us to investigate the upper-tail behaviour of the two models through their survival functions.

44.6.1 CDF of the CSCP marginal

Recall that under the shifted CSCP, \[ \Lambda(u) = \mu + Z(u)^2, \quad Z(u) \sim N(0, \sigma^2) \] and from the previous subsection we showed that

\[ \frac{\Lambda(u) - \mu}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi_1^2 \]

So for any \(\lambda > \mu\), we have

\[ F_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = P(\Lambda(u) \le \lambda) \]

Using the transformation above, this becomes

\[ P(\Lambda(u) \le \lambda) = P\Big( \frac{\Lambda(u) - \mu}{\sigma^2} \le \frac{\lambda - \mu}{\sigma^2} \Big) \]

Since \[ \frac{\Lambda(u) - \mu}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi_1^2 \] it follows that

\[ F_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = F_{\chi_1^2}\Big( \frac{\lambda - \mu}{\sigma^2} \Big), \quad \lambda > \mu \]

Also, since \(\Lambda(u) \ge \mu\) always, we have

\[ F_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = 0, \quad \lambda \le \mu \]

So the full CDF is

\[ F_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = \begin{cases} 0, & \lambda \le \mu, \\ F_{\chi_1^2}\Big( \frac{\lambda - \mu}{\sigma^2} \Big), & \lambda > \mu \end{cases} \]

So, at a fixed location \(u\), the marginal CDF of the CSCP is simply a shifted and scaled chi-square CDF.

44.6.2 CDF of the LGCP marginal

Now recall that under the LGCP, \[ \Lambda(u) = \exp(Z(u)), \quad Z(u) \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2) \]

From the previous subsection, we showed that

\[ \log \Lambda(u) \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2) \]

So for any \(\lambda > 0\), we have

\[ F_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = P(\Lambda(u) \le \lambda) \]

Taking logs of both sides inside the probability gives

\[ P(\Lambda(u) \le \lambda) = P(\log \Lambda(u) \le \log \lambda) \]

But since \[ \log \Lambda(u) \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2) \] this becomes

\[ F_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = P(Z(u) \le \log \lambda) \]

Now standardising,

\[ P(Z(u) \le \log \lambda) = P\Big( \frac{Z(u)-\mu}{\sigma} \le \frac{\log \lambda - \mu}{\sigma} \Big) \]

Since \[ \frac{Z(u)-\mu}{\sigma} \sim N(0,1) \]

it follows that

\[ F_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = \Phi\Big( \frac{\log \lambda - \mu}{\sigma} \Big), \quad \lambda > 0 \]

where \(\Phi\) is the standard normal CDF.

Also, since \(\Lambda(u) = \exp(Z(u)) > 0\) always, we have

\[ F_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = 0, \quad \lambda \le 0 \]

So the full CDF is

\[ F_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = \begin{cases} 0, & \lambda \le 0 \\ \Phi\Big( \frac{\log \lambda - \mu}{\sigma} \Big), & \lambda > 0 \end{cases} \]

So, at a fixed location \(u\), the marginal CDF of the LGCP is the CDF of a lognormal random variable.

44.6.3 Comparison

While the CSCP and LGCP can exhibit very similar second-order structure, their one-point marginal cumulative distributions are fundamentally different (although how different, I feel is not very much).

In particular:

  • the CSCP marginal CDF is that of a shifted and scaled chi-square random variable

  • the LGCP marginal CDF is that of a lognormal random variable

44.6.4 Marginal survival functions

Since we are especially interested in how the two marginals differ in their upper tails, it is natural to also consider the survival functions,

\[ \bar F_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = 1 - F_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) \]

For the CSCP, this gives

\[ \bar F_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = \begin{cases} 1, & \lambda \le \mu \\ 1 - F_{\chi_1^2}\Big( \frac{\lambda - \mu}{\sigma^2} \Big), & \lambda > \mu \end{cases} \]

and for the LGCP,

\[ \bar F_{\Lambda(u)}(\lambda) = \begin{cases} 1, & \lambda \le 0 \\ 1 - \Phi\Big( \frac{\log \lambda - \mu}{\sigma} \Big), & \lambda > 0 \end{cases} \]

44.11 Final Verdict
